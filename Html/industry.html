What Is the IT Industry?

The IT industry is incredibly broad, because it's a lot of things. The absolute basics is just the study of and use of tech systems. You're basically using hardware and software to accomplish whatever the business your working for needs.

For example, say your company needs more computers for the new employees coming in later next month. You could be the one who orders those computers pre-built from another company to supply those new computers. Or you could be the one who builds them yourself depending on what those employees need to do on them.

You could also be the one who trains them on how to use the software they'll need to use if you're more on the software side of things. Heck, you could be the one who codes that very software they're going to be using.

In IT, there's many different approaches to the same problem. How you accomplish them is just up to you or what department you're in.

Subset: The Sysadmin

A subset of the IT industry that I'm particularly interested in is the sysadmin career. The sysadmin basically monitors the company's systems to ensure everything is running smoothly on them. The systems can range from servers, internet infrastructure, to just plain old company PCs.

It's nothing really crazy since the main job is just making sure the systems don't catch on fire, explode, and then die. You're just "keeping the lights on" in a sense. If something does go wrong though, the blame falls on you most of the time.

The duties you'd have would vary depending on who you're working for, but the general expectation is installing operating systems on new computers, installing software on those new operating systems, updating systems to ensure they're always up to date, or upgrading old computers to newer hardware.

Potential Ethical Dilemmas

A potential ethical dilemma could be the common issue of "corner cutting." Say, for example, your supervisor wants you to give full access permissions to Karen in the finance sector. This would mean giving her access to add or delete anything she wanted, or even view the emails of other employees if she was curious enough. The reason your supervisor wants you to do this, is because he thinks it would take too much time to manually set permissions for her. She also had full access before you came along, so she's expecting more of the same.

However, you know that this presents a security risk. If she gets social engineered by an hacker, then the hacker has full access to everything when she gets hacked. After all, it's never "if," but "when" when it comes to attacks. This would bring even more damage and inconvenience than if She was just given normal permissions like everyone else.

Personal Values and Response

How my personal values tie into this potential issue mostly ties to values 1 & 2. My natural response would be to disagree immediately and argue how much of a security risk that is. However, I know that my supervisor is very likely not tech-savvy, and so what I say would likely fly over his head. He'd then argue how valuable Karen is as part of the finance department and how imperative it is that she gets full access. See things from his perspective. Karen is indeed a valuable asset and has done a lot for the company, but that doesn't mean she needs full access for the sake of convenience. Convenience has it's price. I have to take a step back and find a way to explain this in a language he understands.

I'd then carefully explain that I do understand his position, Karen is indeed a very valued employee, however, giving her full access would be a "short term gain, long term loss" situation. Sure, it would make everything convenient for her, but if she were to get hacked? Her getting hacked would inconvenience even more people than just her. It could also potentially cost the company money in suits and damages.

When it comes to those outside your sector, you have to explain it in their language, not your own. The language of money is a language most people in the business easily understand, and the idea of losing more money to a hack in exchange for a more inconvenienced employee is an easy trade to see.

Potential Security Issues

There are several potential issues that could come about. By far, the most common is weak passwords and no multi-factor authentication. Many employees will just use the very simple & easy passwords to make things easier for them. The problem is that while it does make signing in convenient, it comes at the host of weaker security. Many hackers will guess the passwords to try and brute force their way in. They use passwords that are very common first, then move on to passwords you've used on other accounts. If you have a super easy password, your account for work will likely get hacked in seconds.

Best Cybersecurity Practices

1. Only give people access to what they need: Nothing more, nothing less, only give people the minimum access they need to complete their job. Karen in Finance doesn't need to have the ability to read the emails of John in front desk to process monthly earnings for the quarter. Always ask yourself what someone at minimum needs to do their job.

2. Strong Password Policies: Enforce strong password policies where you can. The easiest is to require at least 1 capital letter, one lowercase letter, one unique character, and one number. At least one of each. This makes passwords for everyone infinitely more complex, thus making their accounts harder to hack into by hackers.

3. Always Keep Systems Up to Date: Always ensure that operating systems and software are on the latest stable version. Most of the time, new updates include security patches that fix new vulnerabilities that come out. You should always update when you can so your software is always protected against the latest vulnerabilities.

4. ALWAYS HAVE BACKUPS: This one is extremely important. In the event something does go wrong, always have backups to the company's data so you can put the data back if it gets accidentally deleted or stolen. The same thing with snapshots, always have a snapshot so that if something goes wrong, you can just revert back to a previous version and all's good in the world.
